\begin{abstract}
Music source separation, the recovery of individual stems such as vocals and
accompaniment from a single mixture, is usually approached either by classical
signal-processing decompositions or by large end-to-end neural networks. We show
that a wide span of these methods are instances of one underlying principle:
recovery of structured (sparse or low-rank) representations of the time-frequency
spectrogram. Under this view we construct and evaluate four separators on a common
short-time Fourier transform front end: a low-rank-plus-sparse decomposition by
principal component pursuit, a supervised non-negative matrix factorization with
per-source dictionaries, a discriminative K-SVD dictionary model with
sparse-representation classification, and a deep sparse coding network that
predicts a time-frequency mask by unrolling a non-negative proximal-gradient
solver and learning its dictionaries end-to-end. We further give a joint-sparsity
extension for stereo recovery. On 25 held-out tracks of the MUSDB18 benchmark, the
methods form a clear ordering: training-free baselines (median-filtering harmonic
percussive separation, REPET-SIM) trail supervised dictionary methods, and the
unrolled deep sparse coding network is the strongest non-oracle separator on both
sources (vocal SI-SDR $1.6$ dB, accompaniment SI-SDR $7.7$ dB) while being the
fastest learned model at inference. We connect each method to the recovery theory
that governs it and report a negative result: the learned dictionaries are highly
coherent ($\mu\approx0.85$), so worst-case exact-recovery guarantees are vacuous at
the operating sparsity, yet separation succeeds, an instance of the
average-case-versus-worst-case gap for trained dictionaries. Code, trained models,
and audio are released.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Single-channel music source separation asks for the constituent stems of a
recording given only the final mixture. It underpins remixing, upmixing,
music education, and downstream music-information-retrieval tasks such as
transcription and lyric alignment, and it remains hard because the mixing process
is information-destroying: many stem configurations explain the same
mixture~\cite{cano2019introduction,araki2025thirtyyears}. Progress has come from
two largely separate traditions. The first is model-based: non-negative matrix
factorization (NMF)~\cite{lee1999nmf,smaragdis2007convolutive}, low-rank-plus-sparse
decompositions~\cite{huang2012rpca}, and dictionary
learning~\cite{aharon2006ksvd} exploit explicit structural priors on the
spectrogram. The second is data-driven: deep networks trained end-to-end,
including U-Nets~\cite{jansson2017unet,stoller2018waveunet},
recurrent masking models~\cite{stoter2019openunmix}, and hybrid
waveform/spectrogram architectures~\cite{defossez2021demucs,rouard2023htdemucs},
define the current state of the art.

This paper takes the position that these traditions are two ends of a single
axis. The spectrogram magnitude of a musical source is approximately sparse in a
source-adapted basis (a vocal frame activates a few formant atoms; a percussive
frame is sparse in time) and the sustained harmonic content of an accompaniment is
approximately low-rank. Both properties are exactly the structural assumptions of
compressed sensing and sparse coding~\cite{donoho2006cs,elad2010sparse}. Deep
masking networks, in turn, can be read as unrolled sparse-recovery
solvers with learned operators~\cite{gregor2010lista,monga2021unrolling}. Placing
the classical and deep methods on one short-time Fourier transform (STFT) front
end therefore lets us compare them as points on a structure-recovery spectrum
rather than as unrelated systems.

\paragraph{Contributions.} (i) We formalize vocal/accompaniment separation as a
structured-recovery problem and instantiate four separators that share a front end
and an evaluation harness (\cref{sec:method}). (ii) We construct a deep sparse
coding network that predicts a soft time-frequency mask by unrolling a
non-negative fast proximal-gradient iteration and learning the synthesis
dictionaries end-to-end, and show it is the strongest non-oracle separator in our
study while being the fastest learned model at test time
(\cref{sec:scn,sec:exp}). (iii) We pair every method with the recovery condition
that governs it, and report an honest negative result on dictionary coherence that
quantifies the gap between worst-case guarantees and observed behaviour
(\cref{sec:theory,sec:coherence}). All code, trained weights, and audio examples
are released for reproducibility.

\section{Background: Spectrograms, Stems, and Masking}
\label{sec:background}
We summarize the audio concepts the paper relies on so the development is
self-contained for readers outside music processing.

\paragraph{From waveform to spectrogram.}
A recording is a pressure waveform $y(t)$ sampled at $f_s$ samples per second. The
short-time Fourier transform (STFT) slides a window of length $w$ across the
signal with hop $h$ and takes a discrete Fourier transform in each window,
producing a complex matrix $\Y\in\mathbb C^{F\times T}$ where $F=w/2+1$ frequency
bins index pitch and $T$ frames index time. Its modulus $\M=\lvert\Y\rvert$, the
\emph{magnitude spectrogram}, is a nonnegative image whose brightness at
$(f,t)$ is the energy near frequency $f$ at time $t$; its argument $\angle\Y$ is
the \emph{phase}. The transform is invertible, so $y$ can be reconstructed
exactly from $\M$ and $\angle\Y$.

\paragraph{Stems and the separation goal.}
A finished song is a sum of source \emph{stems} (vocals, drums, bass, other). We
target the two-way split into vocals and accompaniment (everything else). Because
the STFT is linear, the mixture spectrogram is approximately the sum of the source
spectrograms, $\Y\approx\sum_c\Y_c$, and on magnitudes the sources mostly occupy
different time-frequency cells, which is what makes masking viable.

\paragraph{Time-frequency masking.}
Rather than predict a source spectrogram outright, we predict a \emph{mask}
$\M_c\in[0,1]^{F\times T}$ that scales each cell of the mixture and resynthesize
with the mixture phase,
\begin{equation}
\hat y_c=\STFT^{-1}(\M_c\odot\Y).
\end{equation}
A mask answers a local question: what fraction of the energy in cell $(f,t)$
belongs to source $c$? The optimal mask under a squared-error criterion is the
\emph{ideal ratio mask}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:irm}
\M_c^{\mathrm{IRM}}=\frac{\lvert\Y_c\rvert^2}{\sum_k\lvert\Y_k\rvert^2},
\end{equation}
which is exactly the multichannel Wiener / minimum-mean-square-error estimator when
sources are modelled as locally uncorrelated Gaussians. Equation~\eqref{eq:irm}
requires the true sources, so it serves only as an oracle upper bound; the binary
variant $\M_c^{\mathrm{IBM}}=\mathbb 1[\lvert\Y_c\rvert>\lvert\Y_{\neg c}\rvert]$ is a
second oracle. Every method we build estimates a mask without access to the
sources, and no mixture-phase magnitude mask can exceed the ideal-mask ceilings.

\paragraph{Why sparsity and low rank.}
Two empirical regularities make these masks recoverable by structured optimization.
A voiced sound places energy on a sparse set of harmonics (a few bright horizontal
ridges per frame), so its spectrogram is approximately \emph{sparse} in a
harmonic-atom basis. Sustained, repeating accompaniment reuses a small set of
spectral templates across many frames, so its magnitude spectrogram is approximately
\emph{low-rank}. These are the two structural priors compressed sensing is built to
exploit, and they organize the four methods below.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\paragraph{Factorization and low-rank models.}
NMF decomposes a non-negative spectrogram into non-negative spectral atoms and
activations~\cite{lee1999nmf,lee2000algorithms} and was applied to polyphonic
audio by \citet{smaragdis2003nmf}. Supervised and convolutive
variants~\cite{smaragdis2007convolutive,virtanen2007monaural} pre-train one
dictionary per source and hold it fixed at test time, which is the form we adopt.
The choice of divergence matters for audio: the Itakura-Saito
divergence~\cite{fevotte2009isnmf} is scale-invariant and matches a Gaussian
generative model of power spectra. Low-rank methods exploit the repetitive
structure of accompaniment: REPET~\cite{rafii2013repet} models the background as a
repeating pattern, and robust principal component analysis (RPCA) separates a
low-rank accompaniment from a sparse vocal~\cite{candes2011rpca,huang2012rpca}.
Median-filtering harmonic/percussive separation~\cite{fitzgerald2010hpss} is a
training-free baseline in the same family.

\paragraph{Dictionary learning and sparse representation.}
K-SVD learns overcomplete sparsifying dictionaries by alternating sparse coding
with rank-one atom updates~\cite{aharon2006ksvd,engan1999mod}. Sparse-representation
classification (SRC) assigns a signal to the class whose sub-dictionary best
reconstructs it~\cite{wright2009src}; we use this rule to route time-frequency
energy. Task-driven dictionary learning~\cite{mairal2012taskdriven} optimizes the
dictionary for a downstream loss by differentiating through the sparse code, which
is the conceptual bridge to our learned model.

\paragraph{Deep separation and algorithm unrolling.}
End-to-end networks dominate the leaderboards~\cite{hennequin2020spleeter,%
stoter2019openunmix,defossez2021demucs,rouard2023htdemucs,luo2019convtasnet,%
mitsufuji2022mdx}. A complementary line interprets such networks through sparse
coding: LISTA unrolls the iterative shrinkage-thresholding algorithm into a
fixed-depth trainable network~\cite{gregor2010lista}; CNNs admit a multilayer
convolutional-sparse-coding reading~\cite{papyan2017cnncsc}; and algorithm
unrolling is now a general design
principle~\cite{monga2021unrolling,sprechmann2015efficient}. Discriminatively
unfolded NMF has been used for speech separation~\cite{leroux2015deepnmf}, and
supervised deep sparse coding networks have been studied for image
classification~\cite{sun2019scn}. Our learned separator instantiates this
unrolling idea for music spectrogram masking.

\section{Preliminaries: Structured Recovery}
\label{sec:theory}
We collect the recovery results that the methods rely on. Throughout,
$\norm{\xv}_0=\lvert\supp(\xv)\rvert$ counts nonzeros and
$\norm{\xv}_1=\sum_i\lvert x_i\rvert$.

\paragraph{Sparse recovery.}
Given $\A\in\R^{m\times N}$ with $m\ll N$ and $\yv=\A\xv$ for an $s$-sparse
$\xv$, the combinatorial program $\min_{\bm z}\norm{\bm z}_0$ s.t. $\A\bm z=\yv$ is
NP-hard; its convex relaxation $\min_{\bm z}\norm{\bm z}_1$ s.t. $\A\bm z=\yv$ is a
linear program. Exactness of the relaxation is controlled by three quantities. The
\emph{spark} is the size of the smallest linearly dependent column subset, and an
$s$-sparse solution is unique iff $\spark(\A)>2s$. The \emph{restricted isometry
property} (RIP) of order $s$ holds with constant $\delta_s$ if
\begin{equation}
(1-\delta_s)\norm{\xv}_2^2\le\norm{\A\xv}_2^2\le(1+\delta_s)\norm{\xv}_2^2
\end{equation}
for all $s$-sparse $\xv$; $\ell_1$ recovery is exact whenever
$\delta_{2s}<\sqrt{2}-1$~\cite{candes2006rip}. The \emph{mutual coherence}
$\mu(\A)=\max_{i\ne j}\lvert\langle\A_i,\A_j\rangle\rvert$ (unit-norm columns)
gives the verifiable condition
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coh}
s<\tfrac12\!\left(1+\tfrac1{\mu(\A)}\right)
\end{equation}
under which both orthogonal matching pursuit (OMP) and $\ell_1$ minimization
recover every $s$-sparse signal~\cite{tropp2007omp,donoho2006cs}. For
sub-Gaussian random $\A$, RIP holds with high probability once
$m\ge C\,s\log(N/s)$.

\paragraph{Low-rank recovery.}
The nuclear norm $\norm{\X}_*=\sum_i\sigma_i(\X)$ is the convex surrogate for rank.
Robust PCA recovers a low-rank $\X$ and a sparse $\Ev$ from $\M=\X+\Ev$ by
principal component pursuit,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rpca}
\min_{\X,\Ev}\ \norm{\X}_*+\lambda\norm{\Ev}_1
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad \X+\Ev=\M,
\end{equation}
which succeeds with high probability at $\lambda=1/\sqrt{\max(n_1,n_2)}$ when the
low-rank term is incoherent and the support of $\Ev$ is
random~\cite{candes2011rpca,candes2009matrixcompletion}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
\paragraph{Front end.}
Let $y(t)$ be a mono mixture. Its STFT is $\Y=\STFT(y)\in\mathbb C^{F\times T}$
with magnitude $\M=\lvert\Y\rvert$ and phase $\angle\Y$. Every separator produces a
soft mask $\M_c\in[0,1]^{F\times T}$ per source $c$, and resynthesizes
$\hat y_c=\STFT^{-1}(\M_c\odot\Y)$ using the mixture phase. Given non-negative
source magnitude estimates $\widehat\M_c$, we use Wiener-style masks
$\M_c=\widehat\M_c^{2}/\sum_k\widehat\M_k^{2}$.

\subsection{Low-rank plus sparse (RPCA)}
\label{sec:rpca}
We solve \cref{eq:rpca} on the magnitude spectrogram by the inexact augmented
Lagrange multiplier method. With the soft-threshold
$\soft_\tau(x)=\sgn(x)\max(\lvert x\rvert-\tau,0)$ and the singular-value
threshold $\SVT_\tau(\bm Z)=\bm U\soft_\tau(\bm\Sigma)\bm V^\top$ for
$\bm Z=\bm U\bm\Sigma\bm V^\top$, the iteration is
\begin{align}
\X&\leftarrow\SVT_{1/\mu}\!\big(\M-\Ev+\tfrac1\mu\bm\Lambda\big),\\
\Ev&\leftarrow\soft_{\lambda/\mu}\!\big(\M-\X+\tfrac1\mu\bm\Lambda\big),\\
\bm\Lambda&\leftarrow\bm\Lambda+\mu(\M-\X-\Ev),
\end{align}
with $\mu$ increased geometrically. The low-rank $\X$ is the accompaniment estimate
and the sparse $\Ev$ the vocal proxy; this is principal component pursuit applied to
singing-voice separation~\cite{huang2012rpca}. The method is training-free.

\subsection{Supervised NMF}
\label{sec:nmf}
We pre-train one non-negative dictionary per source on isolated stems by minimizing
$\norm{\M_c-\D_c\Hb_c}$ subject to $\D_c,\Hb_c\ge0$. With the
Kullback-Leibler divergence (the natural loss for magnitude
spectra~\cite{fevotte2009isnmf}), the Lee-Seung multiplicative
updates~\cite{lee2000algorithms} are
\begin{equation}
\Hb\leftarrow\Hb\odot\frac{\D^\top(\M\oslash\D\Hb)}{\D^\top\bm1},
\qquad
\D\leftarrow\D\odot\frac{(\M\oslash\D\Hb)\Hb^\top}{\bm1\Hb^\top},
\end{equation}
where $\odot,\oslash$ are elementwise. These updates are not heuristic: they are a
majorization-minimization scheme. For the divergence $D(\M\,\|\,\D\Hb)$ one builds
an auxiliary function $G(\Hb,\Hb')\ge D(\M\,\|\,\D\Hb)$ with equality at
$\Hb'=\Hb$, using the convexity of $-\log$ and Jensen's inequality on the latent
assignment $\D_{ik}H_{kj}/(\D\Hb)_{ij}$; minimizing $G(\cdot,\Hb')$ in closed form
yields the multiplicative step, so each update cannot increase the loss and the
iterates converge to a stationary point. The factors stay nonnegative
automatically because every quantity in the ratio is nonnegative. At test time we
fix $\D=[\D_v\mid\D_a]$ and solve only for activations $\Hb$, then reconstruct
$\widehat\M_v=\D_v\Hb_v$ and $\widehat\M_a=\D_a\Hb_a$. This is the supervised NMF
template of \citet{smaragdis2007convolutive}; the Kullback-Leibler choice
corresponds to a Poisson observation model on spectrogram counts, while the
Itakura-Saito divergence ($\beta\!=\!0$) corresponds to a multiplicative-Gaussian
model of power spectra~\cite{fevotte2009isnmf}.

\subsection{Discriminative dictionaries with SRC}
\label{sec:ksvd}
We replace each NMF dictionary with an overcomplete K-SVD dictionary trained to
sparsely code spectrogram frames, $\min_{\D,\X}\norm{\Y-\D\X}_F^2$ s.t.
$\norm{\xv_i}_0\le s$. K-SVD~\cite{aharon2006ksvd} alternates OMP sparse coding with
a per-atom update: writing $\D\X=\sum_j\D_j\bm x^{j\top}$ and forming the residual
$\Ev_{j}=\Y-\sum_{k\ne j}\D_k\bm x^{k\top}$ restricted to the atom's active set, the
rank-one SVD $\Ev_j^{R}=\bm U\bm\Sigma\bm V^\top$ updates $\D_j\leftarrow\bm u_1$ and
the coefficients $\leftarrow\sigma_1\bm v_1$. The rank-one update is optimal in the
Eckart-Young sense: $(\bm u_1,\sigma_1\bm v_1)$ is the exact minimizer of
$\norm{\Ev_j^{R}-\D_j\bm x^{j\top}}_F$ over all unit atoms and coefficient rows, so
the atom update is a coordinate descent step that cannot increase the objective,
and restricting to the active set $\{i:\bm x^{j}_i\!\ne\!0\}$ preserves the sparsity
pattern set by the coding step. At test time we sparse-code each mixture frame on
$[\D_v\mid\D_a]$ by OMP and route energy by the SRC residual
rule~\cite{wright2009src}: $c^\star=\argmin_c\norm{\yv-\D_c\av_c}_2$. The coherence
condition \cref{eq:coh} governs when this per-frame code is unique, linking the
separator's reliability to a measurable property of the learned dictionary
(\cref{sec:coherence}).

\subsection{Deep sparse coding network}
\label{sec:scn}
Our learned separator predicts the vocal mask by unrolling a non-negative sparse
solver and learning its dictionaries end-to-end. For an input frame $\xv\in\R^F$ a
composite module computes two stacked non-negative elastic-net codes,
\begin{align}
\av^{(1)}&=\argmin_{\av\ge0}\tfrac12\norm{\xv-\D^{(1)}\av}_2^2
+\lambda_1\norm{\av}_1+\tfrac{\lambda_2}2\norm{\av}_2^2,\label{eq:scn1}\\
\av^{(2)}&=\argmin_{\av\ge0}\tfrac12\norm{\av^{(1)}-\D^{(2)}\av}_2^2
+\lambda_1\norm{\av}_1+\tfrac{\lambda_2}2\norm{\av}_2^2,\label{eq:scn2}
\end{align}
with $\D^{(1)}\in\R^{F\times n_u}$ overcomplete ($n_u>F$) and
$\D^{(2)}\in\R^{n_u\times n_d}$ undercomplete ($n_d<n_u$). Each subproblem is solved
by $K$ steps of a non-negative fast proximal-gradient iteration: with step size
$1/L$, $L$ the squared spectral norm of the dictionary (obtained by power
iteration, so no on-device SVD is needed),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fista}
\av_{k}=\big[\bm z_k-\tfrac1L\D^\top(\D\bm z_k-\xv)-\tfrac{\lambda_1}L\big]_+,
\end{equation}
followed by the Nesterov extrapolation
$t_{k+1}=\tfrac12(1+\sqrt{1+4t_k^2})$,
$\bm z_{k+1}=\av_k+\tfrac{t_k-1}{t_{k+1}}(\av_k-\av_{k-1})$. Every operation is a
matrix multiply or a clamp, so the unrolled solver is differentiable and runs on a
GPU. Stacking $L_m$ composite modules and a linear head with a sigmoid yields a
mask $\M_v\in[0,1]^{F\times T}$. The dictionaries $\D^{(\ell)}$ and shrinkage
parameters $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ are trained by backpropagation through the unrolled
iterations, minimizing a masked-magnitude reconstruction loss against the ideal
ratio mask with an explicit sparsity penalty on the codes,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:scnloss}
\mathcal L=\norm{(\M_v-\M_v^{\mathrm{IRM}})\odot\M}_1
+\beta\sum_\ell\norm{\av^{(\ell)}}_1.
\end{equation}
\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Composite module forward pass (one of $L_m$). All steps are matrix
multiplies and clamps, so the pass is differentiable and runs on a GPU.}
\label{alg:scn}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE {\bfseries Input:} frame $\xv$, dictionaries $\D^{(1)},\D^{(2)}$,
shrinkage $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$, steps $K$
\STATE $\D \leftarrow \D^{(1)}$;\; $L\leftarrow \sigma_{\max}(\D)^2+\lambda_2$
\hfill {\small(power iteration)}
\STATE $\av\leftarrow\bm0$;\; $\bm z\leftarrow\bm0$;\; $t\leftarrow1$
\FOR{$k=1$ {\bfseries to} $K$}
  \STATE $\bm g\leftarrow \D^\top(\D\bm z-\xv)+\lambda_2\bm z$
  \hfill {\small(gradient)}
  \STATE $\av^{+}\leftarrow\big[\bm z-\bm g/L-\lambda_1/L\big]_+$
  \hfill {\small(non-neg.\ shrinkage)}
  \STATE $t^{+}\leftarrow\tfrac12(1+\sqrt{1+4t^2})$
  \STATE $\bm z\leftarrow\av^{+}+\tfrac{t-1}{t^{+}}(\av^{+}-\av)$;\;
         $\av\leftarrow\av^{+}$;\; $t\leftarrow t^{+}$
\ENDFOR
\STATE repeat lines 2--9 with $\D^{(2)}$ on input $\av$ to obtain $\av^{(2)}$
\STATE {\bfseries return} $\av^{(2)}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\Cref{alg:scn} gives the per-module computation. \Cref{eq:scn1,eq:scn2,eq:fista}
make explicit that the network is a learned
sparse-recovery operator: \cref{eq:fista} is one unrolled iteration of the same
proximal solver used for $\ell_1$ recovery, with $\D$ promoted to a trainable
parameter, exactly the LISTA construction~\cite{gregor2010lista} specialized to
non-negative codes and a masking output. The clamp $[\cdot]_+$ is the proximal
operator of the indicator of the nonnegative orthant composed with the
soft-threshold, i.e.\ the prox of $\lambda_1\norm{\av}_1+\iota_{\av\ge0}(\av)$, so
each layer is a genuine proximal-gradient step. Run to convergence rather than
truncated, the accelerated iteration attains the optimal first-order rate
$F(\av_k)-F(\av^\star)=O(1/k^2)$~\cite{beck2009fista}; unrolling a fixed $K$ trades
this asymptotic guarantee for a learned operator that reaches comparable quality in
a handful of layers, which is why inference is a single fast forward pass rather
than an iterative solve (\cref{sec:exp}).

\subsection{Joint sparsity for stereo}
\label{sec:mmv}
Stereo channels share active sources, so their codes share a row support. Stacking
the per-frame channel magnitudes into $\Y=[\yv_L\ \yv_R]\in\R^{F\times2}$, we apply
simultaneous OMP~\cite{tropp2006somp}, which scores atoms by the row norm
$\norm{(\D^\top\bm R)_{n,:}}_2$ of the residual and so forces both channels to agree
on the active atoms. The mixed norm $\norm{\X}_{1,2}=\sum_n\norm{\X_{n,:}}_2$ is the
convex surrogate, and the shared support strictly improves identifiability: a
row-$s$-sparse $\X$ is unique once
$s<(\spark(\A)-1+\rank(\Y))/2$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}
\paragraph{Data and protocol.}
We use the MUSDB18 benchmark~\cite{rafii2017musdb18}, separating each mixture into
vocals and accompaniment. Dictionaries and the deep model are trained on the
training split; all numbers are reported on $25$ held-out test tracks at
$22.05$ kHz with a $2048$-point STFT and hop $512$. We report the median
scale-invariant SDR~\cite{leroux2019sdr} and SDR~\cite{vincent2006bsseval} per
source. Two oracle masks bound any masking method: the ideal ratio mask (IRM) and
ideal binary mask (IBM).

\paragraph{Methods.}
We compare two training-free baselines, median-filtering harmonic/percussive
separation (HPSS)~\cite{fitzgerald2010hpss} and REPET-SIM~\cite{rafii2013repet},
against the four structured-recovery separators of \cref{sec:method}: RPCA,
supervised NMF ($K{=}64$ atoms per source, KL divergence), discriminative K-SVD
($K{=}96$, sparsity $s{=}10$), and the deep sparse coding network (SCN; three
composite modules, $n_u{=}192$, $n_d{=}96$, $K{=}18$ unrolled steps, $0.32$M
parameters, trained on Apple MPS).

\paragraph{Main results.}
\Cref{tab:main} and \cref{fig:bars} show a consistent ordering. The trivial floors
calibrate the scale: returning the mixture unchanged already scores $+4.0$ dB on
accompaniment, because the accompaniment dominates the mixture energy, so
accompaniment SI-SDR must be read against this floor rather than against zero; a
random mask is near the no-information floor on both sources. The training-free
baselines are mixed: HPSS over-suppresses tonal vocal energy (its median vocal
SI-SDR falls below even the mixture floor), and REPET-SIM helps only when the
background strictly repeats. The supervised dictionary methods improve sharply over
all floors, and the deep sparse coding network is the best non-oracle separator on
both sources ($1.6$ dB vocals, $7.7$ dB accompaniment) while also being the fastest
learned model, because its forward pass is a fixed number of matrix multiplies
rather than an iterative solve. A simple geometric-mean fusion of the three learned
masks edges out the best single model on accompaniment ($7.8$ dB), indicating the
methods make partly complementary errors. All methods remain below the IRM/IBM
oracle ceilings, as any mixture-phase magnitude mask must.

\paragraph{Per-track variance.}
Medians hide spread. \Cref{fig:box} plots the per-track vocal SI-SDR distribution.
The structured-recovery methods are not uniformly better track by track: their
boxes overlap, and every method has a long lower tail on dense, vocal-quiet
mixtures where the sparsity prior is weakest. The deep model has the highest median
and upper quartile, and shifts the whole distribution rightward relative to the
classical methods rather than only improving easy tracks.

\paragraph{Soft versus binary masks.}
The masking strategy matters independently of the model. \Cref{fig:mask} compares
each learned method's soft (ratio) mask against its hard-thresholded binary version.

($1.6$ vs $0.6$ dB), because a continuous gain preserves the partial-overlap cells
that a binary decision discards. This mirrors the gap between the IRM and IBM
oracles and motivates our soft-mask training target.

\paragraph{Ablations.}
\Cref{tab:abl} sweeps the central hyperparameters and the trends are informative
rather than uniform. For supervised NMF the vocal SI-SDR is \emph{highest at the
smallest dictionary} ($K{=}16$) and declines as $K$ grows: with limited training
data a small dictionary acts as a stronger prior and generalizes better, a
regularization effect rather than a capacity limit. K-SVD separation improves with
the per-frame sparsity budget up to $s{=}10$ and then saturates, so richer codes
help up to the point where added atoms stop explaining new source-specific
structure. For RPCA the vocal SI-SDR increases monotonically with the penalty
$\lambda$: a larger penalty pushes more energy into the low-rank accompaniment and
leaves a cleaner sparse vocal residual, so the canonical
$\lambda{=}1/\sqrt{\max(F,T)}$ is conservative for the singing-voice task even
though it is the value certified for exact low-rank/sparse recovery.

\subsection{Coherence: a negative result}
\label{sec:coherence}
The recovery condition \cref{eq:coh} requires low coherence, but learned
overcomplete dictionaries are built for reconstruction, not incoherence.
\Cref{fig:coh} reports the off-diagonal Gram histogram of the K-SVD vocal
dictionary: the coherence is $\mu\approx0.85$, so \cref{eq:coh} certifies exact
recovery only for $s<\tfrac12(1+1/\mu)\approx1.1$, far below the $s{=}10$ at which
the method operates well. The worst-case guarantee is therefore vacuous in this
regime, yet separation succeeds. This is the expected average-case-versus-worst-case
gap: coherence bounds the worst sparse vector, while typical music frames are far
from that adversarial case. We report it because it sets honest expectations for how
much of the classical theory transfers to trained audio dictionaries.

\centering

worst-case recovery bound certifies only $s\lesssim1$, but separation at $s{=}10$
works, evidence of an average-case-versus-worst-case gap.}

\end{figure}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discuss}
The results are deliberately scoped: short clips, modest dictionaries, and a
$0.32$M-parameter network trained briefly on a laptop GPU. Large end-to-end systems
reach substantially higher vocal SI-SDR with three to four orders of magnitude more
parameters and data~\cite{rouard2023htdemucs,mitsufuji2022mdx}. The value here is
the unifying view and the controlled comparison: on one front end, the same axis of
increasing structure-learning, from training-free decomposition, to supervised
factorization, to discriminative dictionaries, to an unrolled deep solver, produces
a monotone improvement, and the unrolled solver is both the most accurate and the
cheapest to run at inference. Two limitations are intrinsic to the present setup.
First, magnitude masking with mixture phase upper-bounds every method at the IRM
oracle; a complex-mask or phase-reconstruction extension would lift the
ceiling~\cite{luo2019convtasnet}. Second, the deep model is trained per frame and
ignores temporal context; convolutional or recurrent composite modules are a
natural next step. The stereo joint-sparsity extension of \cref{sec:mmv} also
remains to be evaluated at scale.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a unified compressed-sensing view of music source separation, casting
low-rank decomposition, supervised NMF, discriminative dictionary learning, and a
deep unrolled sparse coder as points on one structure-recovery spectrum over the
STFT. On MUSDB18 the methods order cleanly, the unrolled deep sparse coding network
is the strongest non-oracle separator while being the fastest learned model, and a
coherence analysis delimits where the classical recovery theory does and does not
transfer to learned audio dictionaries. Code, models, and audio are released.
