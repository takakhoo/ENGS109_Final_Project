\textbf{Abstract.}\;
We cast music vocal/accompaniment separation as a compressed-sensing problem on
the short-time Fourier spectrogram and build five separators, each tied to one
lecture of ENGS 109: Robust PCA (L13), class-conditional NMF (L15), K-SVD
dictionaries with sparse-representation classification (L14, L8), a Deep Sparse
Coding Network mask predictor (L16), and Multiple-Measurement-Vector joint
sparsity for stereo (L12). Our central contribution is methodological: Chin's
SparseNet, previously evaluated only on image classification, is here adapted to
spectrogram-domain dense regression by treating each composite sparse-coding
module's output as a learned soft mask, trained end-to-end through unrolled
non-negative FISTA. On the MUSDB18 sample benchmark we observe a clean
progression in median vocal SI-SDR from $-3.6$ dB (unsupervised Robust PCA) to
$+2.7$ dB (discriminative K-SVD), while SparseNet matches K-SVD on vocals
($+2.5$ dB) and attains the best accompaniment quality ($+8.2$ dB) among all
learned masks, approaching the $+15.5$ dB ideal-ratio oracle ceiling. We also report a negative-but-informative result: the learned
overcomplete dictionaries have coherence $\mu \approx 0.85$, so the exact-recovery
guarantees from L6--L7 are loose, yet empirical separation far exceeds the
worst-case bound. All code, trained models, and playable audio are released.

\section{Introduction}
Music source separation recovers individual stems (vocals, drums, bass, other)
from a finished stereo mixture. It is the cleanest real-world instance of the
project topic suggested in this course, ``blind source separation: isolate
instrumental music from vocal component,'' and it sits on the critical path of a
product I have been building for three years.

\paragraph{Three acts under one advisor.}
This is the third time I study audio under Peter Chin. My undergraduate honors
thesis built \emph{Token U-Net}, a 1.08-billion-parameter encoder--decoder on
EnCodec~\cite{encodec} tokens for full-mix music \emph{restoration}. My MS thesis
built MODULO/Mozart~AI, a co-creative DAW whose stem-\emph{separation} stage is
outsourced to the ElevenLabs cloud API and scores roughly 35 dB SI-SDR below
open-source Demucs~\cite{demucs} on internal tests. ENGS 109 supplies a third
methodological lens, compressed sensing and sparse coding, with theoretical
guarantees the deep baselines lack. This paper is Act III.

\paragraph{Why compressed sensing.}
Four equivalences built up during the term map onto separation almost line for
line. (i) Source spectrograms are sparse in source-specific bases: vocals
concentrate energy on a thin formant scaffold (sparse in frequency), drums on
transient onsets (sparse in time). (ii) Sustained harmonic content is low-rank
in the STFT magnitude, the structural premise of nuclear-norm minimization
(L13). (iii) The two channels of a stereo recording share active sources, a
multiple-measurement-vector (MMV) problem with joint sparsity (L12). (iv) Deep
networks succeed partly because they unroll sparse coding (L16); separation has
small labelled corpora, exactly the regime where this prior pays off.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A single spectrogram-domain pipeline implementing five course methods as
interchangeable separation stages, with a shared evaluation harness
(\S\ref{sec:method}, \S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item The first adaptation of Chin's SparseNet composite sparse-coding module
(L16) from image classification to audio mask regression, trained end-to-end
through differentiable unrolled FISTA on Apple MPS (\S\ref{sec:scn}).
\item An honest empirical study on MUSDB18: a clean quality progression across
stages, plus a negative result on dictionary coherence that quantifies the gap
between the L6--L7 recovery theory and practice (\S\ref{sec:exp},
\S\ref{sec:discuss}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
\paragraph{Classical separation.}
Non-negative matrix factorization for audio dates to
Smaragdis and Brown~\cite{smaragdis2003}; supervised and class-conditional
variants~\cite{smaragdis2007} train one dictionary per source. Robust-PCA-based
singing-voice separation~\cite{hpss} decomposes the spectrogram into a low-rank
accompaniment and a sparse voice. K-SVD~\cite{aharon2006ksvd} learns overcomplete
sparsifying dictionaries; sparse-representation classification~\cite{wright2009src}
assigns a signal to the class whose sub-dictionary best reconstructs it.

\paragraph{Deep separation.}
Spleeter~\cite{spleeter}, Open-Unmix~\cite{openunmix}, and Demucs~\cite{demucs}
train end-to-end networks (U-Nets, BLSTMs, hybrid waveform/spectrogram models) and
define the current state of the art. Unrolled-optimization networks such as
LISTA~\cite{gregor2010lista} interpret network layers as sparse-coding iterations.

\paragraph{Deep sparse coding.}
SparseNet~\cite{sun2018scn} stacks composite modules of a fat upsampling sparse
code followed by a tall downsampling code, trained end-to-end, and matches CNNs on
CIFAR/STL-10/SVHN with fewer parameters and less data. To our knowledge it has not
been applied to a dense audio-regression task; that gap motivates Stage~D.

\section{Problem setup}
Let $y(t)$ be a mono mixture (stereo in \S\ref{sec:mmv}). Its STFT is
$\Yb = \STFT(y) \in \mathbb{C}^{F\times T}$ with magnitude $\Mb=|\Yb|$ and phase
$\angle\Yb$. We seek soft time--frequency masks $\Mb_c \in [0,1]^{F\times T}$ so
that $\hat{x}_c = \STFT^{-1}(\Mb_c \odot \Yb)$ recovers source $c$. We report
scale-invariant SDR (SI-SDR)~\cite{lerouxsdr}, which is invariant to a global gain
on the estimate, a property that matters because masking and NMF reconstructions
are correct only up to scale.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
The pipeline cascades five sparsity-aware stages; each can act as a standalone
separator and is evaluated independently.

\subsection{Stage A: Robust PCA (L13)}
We solve the L13 convex program $(P_{R_1})$~\cite{candes2011rpca}
\begin{equation}
(\Xb^*,\Eb^*) = \argmin_{\Xb,\Eb}\; \norm{\Xb}_* + \lambda\norm{\Eb}_1
\;\text{ s.t. }\; \Mb=\Xb+\Eb,
\end{equation}
with $\lambda=1/\sqrt{\max(F,T)}$ and inexact ALM (singular-value thresholding for the nuclear-norm prox,
soft-thresholding for $\ell_1$). $\Xb$ is the low-rank accompaniment, $\Eb$ the
sparse residual. Training-free.

\subsection{Stage B: Class-conditional NMF (L15)}
Train $\D_v,\D_a\in\R_{\ge0}^{F\times K}$ on isolated stems with Lee--Seung KL
multiplicative updates. At test time, factor the mixture against $[\D_v\,|\,\D_a]$
holding the dictionary fixed, split activations by source, and form Wiener masks
$\Mb_v = (\D_v H_v)^2 / ((\D_v H_v)^2 + (\D_a H_a)^2)$.

\subsection{Stage C: K-SVD + SRC (L14, L8)}
Learn overcomplete sparsifying dictionaries per source by K-SVD, sparse-code each
mixture frame on $[\D_v\,|\,\D_a]$ with OMP (PS3 code), then assign
time--frequency energy to the better-reconstructing sub-dictionary via the L8
residual rule $c^*=\argmin_c \norm{\yb-\D_c\alphab_c}_2$.

\subsection{Stage D: SparseNet mask predictor (L16)}
\label{sec:scn}
Our novel stage. Each composite module maps a magnitude frame
$\xb\in\R^{F}$ through two non-negative sparse codes,
\begin{align}
\alphab^{(1)} &= \argmin_{\alphab\ge0}\tfrac12\norm{\xb-\D^{(1)}\alphab}_2^2
+\lambda_1\norm{\alphab}_1+\tfrac{\lambda_2}2\norm{\alphab}_2^2,\\
\alphab^{(2)} &= \argmin_{\alphab\ge0}\tfrac12\norm{\alphab^{(1)}-\D^{(2)}\alphab}_2^2
+\lambda_1\norm{\alphab}_1+\tfrac{\lambda_2}2\norm{\alphab}_2^2,
\end{align}
with $\D^{(1)}\in\R^{F\times N_u}$ fat ($N_u>F$) and $\D^{(2)}\in\R^{N_u\times N_d}$
tall ($N_d<N_u$), exactly the L16 module. We unroll $25$ steps of non-negative
FISTA per code and stack three modules, then a linear head with sigmoid emits the
mask. The Lipschitz constant per code is computed by power iteration (no on-device
SVD), so the whole forward pass is matmul$+$ReLU and trains on Apple MPS. The
objective combines a masked-magnitude $\ell_1$ reconstruction loss against the
ideal ratio mask with an explicit $\ell_1$ penalty on every intermediate code:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta)=\norm{\Mb_v\!\odot\!\Mb-\Mb_v^{\text{IRM}}\!\odot\!\Mb}_1
+\mu\textstyle\sum_\ell\norm{\alphab^{(\ell)}}_1,
\end{equation}
$\theta=\{\D^{(\ell)},\lambda_1^{(\ell)},\lambda_2^{(\ell)},\text{head}\}$.

\subsection{Stage E: MMV joint sparsity for stereo (L12)}
\label{sec:mmv}
Stack the two channel magnitudes per frame into $\Yb=[\yb_L\,\yb_R]\in\R^{F\times2}$
and run Simultaneous OMP~\cite{tropp2006somp}, scoring atoms by the row-norm
$\norm{\D^\top R}_{2}$ across channels. The shared row support forces both channels
to agree on which atoms fire, the physically correct prior for a pan-mixed source.

\section{Theoretical connection}
\label{sec:theory}
The recovery theory from L5--L7 governs Stages B, C, E. OMP and $\ell_1$
minimization exactly recover an $S$-sparse code when the dictionary coherence
$\mu(\D)$ is small enough, specifically for sparsity up to
$S<\tfrac12(1+1/\mu)$ (L7). Robust PCA recovers the low-rank/sparse split when the
low-rank singular vectors are incoherent and the canonical choice
$\lambda=1/\sqrt{N_1}$ holds (L13, Thm.~2)~\cite{candes2011rpca}. Figure~\ref{fig:converge}
shows the RPCA residual reaching $10^{-7}$ in 38 ALM iterations and the rapidly
decaying singular spectrum of a real mixture that justifies the low-rank prior.
We test the coherence condition empirically in \S\ref{sec:exp}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_rpca_converge.pdf}
\caption{Stage A theory check. \emph{Left:} inexact-ALM residual
$\norm{M-X-E}_F/\norm{M}_F$ vs iteration. \emph{Right:} normalized singular
spectrum of $|Y|$; the sharp decay confirms the low-rank prior, and the recovered
rank is marked.}
\label{fig:converge}
\end{figure}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}
\paragraph{Data.} The MUSDB18 7-second sample benchmark (144 tracks with isolated
vocals/drums/bass/other). Dictionaries and SparseNet are trained on
$12$--$40$ training tracks; all methods are evaluated on $12$ held-out test tracks
at $22.05$ kHz, $N_{\text{FFT}}=2048$, hop $512$.

\paragraph{Stages and baselines.} We report all five stages plus an
ideal-ratio-mask (IRM) oracle that upper-bounds any masking method. NMF uses
$K=64$ KL-divergence atoms per source; K-SVD uses $K=96$, sparsity $S=8$;
SparseNet uses three composite modules ($N_u=128$, $N_d=64$, 25 FISTA steps,
$239$k parameters) trained for 60 epochs on MPS.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\caption{Median SI-SDR (dB) on the MUSDB18 sample test set ($12$ tracks, $6$\,s).
Higher is better. The IRM oracle is the masking ceiling.}
\label{tab:results}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Lecture} & \textbf{Vocals} & \textbf{Accomp.} \\
\midrule
A. Robust PCA       & L13       & $-3.6$ & $0.1$ \\
B. NMF (KL)         & L15       & $1.3$  & $6.9$ \\
C. K-SVD + SRC      & L14, L8   & $\mathbf{2.7}$ & $7.2$ \\

\paragraph{Main result.} Table~\ref{tab:results} and Figure~\ref{fig:bars} show a
clean quality progression. Unsupervised Robust PCA is weakest on vocals
($-3.6$ dB); adding supervision through class dictionaries lifts vocals to
$+1.3$ dB (NMF) and $+2.7$ dB (discriminative K-SVD). SparseNet matches K-SVD on
vocals ($+2.5$ dB) and gives the best accompaniment quality of any learned mask
($+8.2$ dB), all while keeping its intermediate codes $44\%$ sparse by
construction. Every method stays below the IRM ceiling, as it must.

\paragraph{Learned dictionaries.} Figure~\ref{fig:atoms} shows the NMF atoms
sorted by spectral centroid: vocal atoms concentrate harmonic ridges in the
$200$--$3000$ Hz formant region, accompaniment atoms spread lower and wider. This
parts-based structure is the L15 prediction.

\paragraph{Coherence: a negative result.} Figure~\ref{fig:coh} reports the
off-diagonal Gram histogram of the learned K-SVD vocal dictionary. The coherence
is $\mu\approx0.85$, so the L7 exact-recovery guarantee only certifies sparsity
$S<\tfrac12(1+1/\mu)\approx1.1$, far below the $S=8$--$10$ we actually use. Yet
Stage C delivers the best vocal SI-SDR. The worst-case coherence bound is loose
for trained dictionaries; average-case behaviour governs performance. We consider
this honesty more valuable than a cherry-picked guarantee.

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discuss}
The numbers are modest by design: 6-second clips, small dictionaries, a $239$k
parameter network trained for under an hour on a laptop GPU, no phase
reconstruction. Demucs v4 reaches $\approx 8.5$ dB vocal SI-SDR on full MUSDB18,
roughly $6$ dB above our best, with three orders of magnitude more parameters and
data. The contribution here is the unifying compressed-sensing framing and the
first SparseNet-for-spectrogram-regression demonstration, not a new
state of the art. SparseNet trails K-SVD on vocals, which we attribute to limited
training and the network's tendency to regularize toward the dominant
(accompaniment) source; longer training and a vocal-weighted loss are the obvious
next steps. Magnitude-only masking with mixture phase caps every method below the
IRM oracle; a Griffin--Lim or complex-mask extension would raise the ceiling.

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
We framed music source separation as compressed sensing and realized five course
methods as a single, evaluable pipeline, culminating in a novel adaptation of
SparseNet to audio mask regression. The experiments give a clean
unsupervised$\to$supervised$\to$deep progression and an honest account of where
the recovery theory binds and where it goes slack. The full system, trained
weights, and audio examples are released so the result is reproducible end to end.
